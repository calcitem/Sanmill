Pick one fix — read only the one that sounds like you

Fix A: You keep running out of moves

This is a mobility problem. You are winning material or mills but ending up boxed in.

What to do:
Before you move, count how many squares your own pieces can reach. If that number is going down every turn, stop and find a move that keeps it stable. A cramped position that still has six legal moves beats a "good" position with three.

Fix B: You can't create any real threats

This is a structure problem. Your pieces are scattered or all on one side.

What to do:
In the placing phase, put your first few pieces on connector points — squares that touch more than two lines. Do not fill a ring completely on one side. Spread across rings so you have two independent threat directions.

Fix C: You form a mill and still lose

This is a tempo problem. You are spending moves to open and close the same mill while the opponent improves position.

What to do:
Before you rock a mill open again, ask whether the piece you will capture matters. If the opponent can replace the captured piece with something better placed, the mill is costing you more than it is earning.

Four short drills

Each drill targets one mistake. Do them in order across four sessions, not all at once.

1

Play three games. Your only goal: at the end of every turn your pieces have at least as many legal moves as they did before.

2

Play three games. Only form a mill if you can also threaten a second mill on the same turn.

3

Play three games focusing on the pieces you place, not the mills you might form. Place on connectors and cross-ring points first.

4

After any loss, find the exact turn when your piece count of legal moves first dropped below your opponent's. That is where the game was decided.

What's going wrong — quick diagnosis

Moves that feel smart but are not

  • Capturing any piece just because you can — not all captures help.
  • Defending the last attacked point reactively every turn.
  • Breaking a strong structure to get one mill and then finding yourself with nothing.
  • Placing pieces without thinking about what ring they connect to.

Habits that actually change results

  • Asking "what does this move do to the whole board" not just the nearby squares.
  • Prioritising captures that reduce opponent mobility, not just piece count.
  • Keeping at least two independent threat directions alive at once.
  • Treating a blocked position as the real loss — piece count is secondary.

One sentence to remember

The opponent does not need to capture all your pieces — they only need to take away your last legal move.

Everything else in this page is a consequence of that fact. If you understand why blocking wins, the rest follows.

When the AI feels "impossible"

It is usually not the AI's tactics that beat you. It is the quiet pressure: pieces slowly herded into corners, options gradually removed.

Run drill 4 once. Find the exact turn. Most players discover the game was decided three or four moves earlier than they thought — and the losing move looked perfectly fine at the time.
